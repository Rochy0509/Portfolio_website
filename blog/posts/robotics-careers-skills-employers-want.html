Robotics Careers:
Essential Skills Employers Want in 2025

July 9, 2025 · 10 min read · Kenneth Martinez

The robotics job market in 2025 is different from 2020. Companies aren't just hiring "ROS developers" anymore — they want full-stack robotics engineers who can bridge simulation, embedded systems, perception, and deployment.

I've interviewed at and worked with companies from early-stage startups to established players (agritech, surgical robotics, humanoid). Here's what actually gets you hired.

Technical Core (Non-Negotiable)

1. ROS2 (Humble/Iron) — Not ROS1

ROS1 is EOL. Every serious company has migrated or is migrating. You need:

  • ros2_control — hardware interfaces, controller_manager, real-time loops
  • Composition — components, intra-process comms, lifecycle nodes
  • QoS policies — reliability vs durability for sensor vs command topics
  • DDS tuning — Fast DDS / Cyclone DDS configuration for real-time

2. C++17/20 + Modern CMake

Python is for prototyping. Production robotics runs on C++. You must be comfortable with:

  • Smart pointers, RAII, move semantics
  • Templates, concepts (C++20)
  • CMake: FetchContent, targets, toolchains for cross-compilation
  • Real-time safe code: no allocations, no locks in control loops

3. Simulation & Digital Twins

Hardware is expensive and slow. Simulation-first development is standard:

  • Gazebo / Ignition — SDF, plugins, sensor simulation
  • Isaac Sim — GPU-accelerated, photorealistic, ROS2 bridge
  • MuJoCo — fast dynamics for RL/MPC research
  • Domain randomization, sim-to-real transfer

4. Embedded & Real-Time

The robot is the hardware. Know your microcontrollers:

  • STM32 (CubeIDE, HAL, FreeRTOS, ThreadX)
  • Micro-ROS / ros2_control on MCU
  • CAN bus, EtherCAT, SPI, I2C — protocol level debugging
  • Bootloaders, OTA updates, safety certification basics (ISO 13849)

Soft Skills That Actually Matter

  • Systems thinking — trace a bug from sensor noise → filter → planner → controller → actuator
  • Documentation — your URDF, launch files, and param configs are read by 5+ engineers
  • Hardware respect — you break a $15k arm, you own the post-mortem
  • Cross-team comms — mechanical, electrical, firmware, ML — you're the bridge
"The best robotics engineers I've hired didn't have the most publications — they had the most scars from hardware integration."

Portfolio Projects That Get Interviews

Skip the "line follower" and "obstacle avoider." Build these instead:

  • Manipulation: Pick-and-place with MoveIt2 + real gripper + force control
  • Navigation: SLAM + Nav2 + custom behavior tree for dynamic obstacles
  • Sim-to-real: Train policy in Isaac Sim, deploy on real robot with domain randomization
  • Full stack: MCU (STM32) → ros2_control → MoveIt2 → Web UI (Foxglove/ROS2 bridge)
